Quick answer: how much do I need to retire in India?

At ~6% Indian inflation, you need roughly 25–30× your annual expenses as a starting corpus for a normal 25–30 year retirement, and 35–40× for a 50-year early-retirement (FIRE) horizon. So spending ₹1 Lakh/month (₹12 L/year) means about ₹3–3.6 Crore for normal retirement, ₹4–5 Crore for early retirement.

These are rules of thumb — the calculator below runs your exact numbers under FY 2025-26 New Regime tax (Section 87A rebate, Section 80TTB, LTCG 12.5% with ₹1.25 L exemption), 12% separate healthcare inflation, and 500 Monte Carlo runs of equity volatility (σ ≈ 18%). You get the exact age your money runs out, the probability of success, and a side-by-side comparison of five strategies: pure FD, 50/50, SCSS at 8.2%, Arbitrage + SWP, and lifetime annuity.

Glossary

Plain-language definitions

Every financial term used on this page, defined in one sentence. Marked up as DefinedTermSet schema so AI assistants can quote definitions accurately.

Withdrawal Rate (WD rate)
The percentage of your remaining corpus you pull out each year to fund expenses. The classic safe withdrawal rate is 4% (Bengen 1994); for India with higher inflation, 3.5% is more realistic.
Corpus
The total pot of money you have. Nominal corpus is the rupee number on your statement; real corpus is the same money expressed in today's purchasing power after inflation.
FD (Fixed Deposit)
A bank/NBFC term deposit. Returns 5.5–7.5% in India (May 2026), fully taxable as "income from other sources" at your slab rate.
SCSS (Senior Citizen Savings Scheme)
Government-of-India scheme for ages 60+ paying 8.2% per annum (Q1 FY 25-26), max ₹30 L per individual (₹60 L per couple), quarterly payout, 5-year tenure extendable by 3 years. India Post source.
SWP (Systematic Withdrawal Plan)
A scheduled withdrawal from a mutual fund. You sell a fixed rupee amount of units each month; only the gains portion is taxed (LTCG 12.5% beyond ₹1.25 L for equity, slab rate for debt).
LTCG (Long-Term Capital Gains)
On equity held more than 1 year, tax is 12.5% with a ₹1.25 L annual exemption — changed from 10% / ₹1 L by Finance Act 2024 (Budget 2024).
Section 87A Rebate
Under FY 2025-26 New Regime, total income up to ₹12 L pays zero income tax thanks to this rebate, with marginal relief for amounts just above the ceiling.
Section 80TTB
Senior citizens (60+) can deduct up to ₹50,000 of interest income (FD, savings, post office) from taxable income each year.
FRSB (RBI Floating Rate Savings Bonds)
7-year tenure RBI bond. Rate floats with NSC + 35 bps (currently 8.05%, Jan–Jun 2026), interest paid semi-annually, fully taxable at slab rate.
Annuity (Lifetime)
You pay an insurer a lumpsum; they pay you a fixed monthly income for life (LIC Jeevan Akshay-style). Safe but inflexible — no inflation adjustment, money "dies" with you unless return-of-purchase option is taken.
Monte Carlo Simulation
Instead of using one "average" return, the calculator runs the same plan 500 times with random returns drawn from a normal distribution (mean μ, standard deviation σ). The output is a probability your plan succeeds, not a single answer.
Sequence-of-Returns Risk
When you're withdrawing, the order of returns matters. A 30% crash in year 2 of retirement is far worse than the same crash in year 20, even when the long-run average is identical.
Retirement calculator
A tool that projects how long a lumpsum lasts given monthly spending, returns, inflation, and taxes. This one outputs runway in years, depletion age, real purchasing power at the end, and probability of success (Monte Carlo).
How much do I need to retire in India? (rule of thumb)
At ~6% Indian inflation, aim for 25–30× annual expenses for a 25–30 year retirement and 35–40× for ~50 years (FIRE). At ₹1 L/month (₹12 L/year): roughly ₹3–3.6 Cr normal retirement, ₹4–5 Cr early retirement. Use the calculator for your exact numbers.
Healthcare inflation (India)
Indian healthcare costs rise at 12–14% per year — roughly twice the general consumer-price inflation of 5–6%. By year 20, your hospital bill at age 80 may be 12–15× current cost.
Real vs. Nominal returns
Nominal is the headline rupee number. Real is the same number after subtracting inflation, i.e. in today's purchasing power. ₹10 Cr in 30 years at 6% inflation has the purchasing power of only ₹1.74 Cr today.
Frequently asked questions

Common questions

Answers to questions we hear most. (Also marked-up as FAQPage schema so AI assistants can quote them accurately.)

Will my money last my whole retirement?

That depends on three numbers: how much you have, how much you spend, and how long you live. This calculator does the maths year-by-year on those three inputs.

Rough rule of thumb at 6% Indian inflation: you need roughly 25-30× your annual expenses as a starting corpus for a normal 25-30 year retirement, and 35-40× for an early-retirement horizon of 50 years.

So if you spend ₹1 Lakh/month (₹12 L/year): ₹3-3.6 Crore for a normal retirement, ₹4-5 Crore for early retirement. Type your real numbers above to get the exact answer for your situation.

When will my money run out, exactly?

The calculator gives the exact year and exact age at which your money hits zero. Look at the "Depletes at age" stat card at the top of the calculator section.

If it shows a number (e.g., 78), that's the age at which your corpus is projected to hit zero. If it says "Never", your money survives the full simulated horizon (default 50 years). The verdict callout below names the depletion age explicitly.

How much can I safely spend each month?

The classic "4% rule" from William Bengen's 1994 study says you can safely withdraw 4% of your starting corpus per year (inflation-adjusted) for at least 30 years.

For India, with higher inflation, 3.5% is more realistic. So a ₹3 Crore corpus supports roughly ₹10.5 Lakh per year, or ₹87,500 per month.

The best test: increase the "Monthly expense" slider until the "Depletes at age" number drops below your expected life expectancy. The biggest number that still gives you comfort is your sustainable spend.

What does this retirement calculator do?

It takes your current savings, expected returns, monthly expenses, inflation, and tax assumptions, then simulates year-by-year what happens over a horizon of up to 60 years.

You get: how many years your money lasts, the exact age at which your savings hit zero (if they do), how much you'll have at the end (in both future and today's purchasing power), and the probability of success under realistic random equity returns (Monte Carlo, 500 runs).

Five strategies are compared on the same inputs: Pure FD, 50/50 FD + Equity, SCSS-led (annual rebalance), Arbitrage + SWP (tax-efficient), and Lifetime Annuity.

What is "withdrawal rate" (WD rate)?

Withdrawal rate = (amount you draw this year) ÷ (corpus at start of year), expressed as a percentage. It tells you how hard your corpus is working.

The classic 4% safe withdrawal rate comes from William Bengen's 1994 study — withdrawing 4% of starting corpus each year (inflation-adjusted) lasts at least 30 years with 50/50 stock-bond allocation, across all historical US market periods including the Great Depression and 1970s stagflation.

For India, with structurally higher inflation, 3.5% is a more honest safe rate. If your WD rate trends above 5–6% for sustained periods, your plan is fragile.

What are the five strategies and which one is best for me?

1. 50/50 FD + Equity — Half in fixed deposit, half in stock-market equity. Live off the FD's interest; once it runs out, sell half the equity to refill. Simple. Tax-inefficient.

2. SCSS-led (annual rebalance) — For senior citizens (60+). First ₹30 L (₹60 L if spouse on board) in SCSS @ 8.2% (sovereign-backed). Top up from equity once a year using ₹1.25 L LTCG tax-free exemption. Higher yield, lower tax than option 1. Always strictly better if you're eligible.

3. Arbitrage + SWP — Park "safe" bucket in arbitrage mutual funds (taxed like equity, not as interest income). Set up a Systematic Withdrawal Plan for monthly income. Best for high tax brackets (30%) with large corpus.

4. Pure FD (no equity) — Everything in FD. Zero market risk, but inflation destroys real purchasing power over 20+ years. Useful as a baseline; rarely the right answer.

5. Lifetime annuity — Trade your money for a guaranteed monthly income for life from an insurance company. Cannot run out. But the payout is fixed forever (no inflation adjustment), money is locked up, and rates depend on age at purchase.

Which is best? For most people aged 55-65 with ₹2 Cr+, strategy #2 (SCSS-led). For younger or higher-bracket people, #3 (Arbitrage + SWP). For maximum safety with zero decisions, #5 (Annuity). Strategies #1 and #4 are baselines — useful for comparison but rarely the right answer.

What's the difference between "nominal" and "real" corpus?

Nominal is the rupee number you'd see on your bank statement in the future. Real is the same money expressed in today's purchasing power after inflation.

At 6% inflation, ₹10 Cr in 30 years has the purchasing power of only ₹1.74 Cr today. The calculator always shows both — but the real number is the one that matters for life planning.

What is Monte Carlo simulation and why does it matter?

Instead of using one "average" equity return (say 12%), Monte Carlo runs the same plan 500 times with randomly varying returns drawn from a normal distribution: mean = your μ (12%), standard deviation = your σ (18% for Indian equity historically).

The output isn't a single answer — it's a probability. Something like "78% chance your plan succeeds."

This matters because of sequence-of-returns risk: a 30% crash in year 2 of retirement is devastating; the same crash in year 20 is mostly harmless. A simple "average return" calculation hides this. Monte Carlo exposes it.

• 0% up to ₹4 L · 5% to ₹8 L · 10% to ₹12 L · 15% to ₹16 L · 20% to ₹20 L · 25% to ₹24 L · 30% above

• LTCG 12.5% on equity beyond ₹1.25 L exemption (Budget 2024)

Why is healthcare inflation separate from general inflation?

In India, healthcare costs inflate at 12-14% per year — roughly twice general consumer-price inflation. For retirees this matters enormously: by year 20 your hospital bill at age 80 may be 12-15× current cost, even as general goods cost only 3-4× more.

Using a single CPI number for everything systematically underestimates healthcare risk. The calculator inflates your health insurance premium and reserve separately at the faster rate — which is the honest thing to do.

How do life events work?

You can schedule any number of one-off expenses at future years — child's wedding, MBA abroad, big medical, car replacement, international travel, etc.

Each event is entered in today's rupees (the calculator inflates it to future rupees automatically) at the year you expect to spend it. In that year, the calculator subtracts the amount from your corpus (drawn first from FD, then from equity with LTCG tax computed on the equity portion).

You can drag-reorder the events list — the order is just for your organisation; the actual timing comes from the "year" field.

Is the output financial advice?

No. This is an illustrative simulation tool. It uses simplifying assumptions — constant or randomly-distributed returns, fixed tax law, no idiosyncratic shocks like job loss, divorce, fraud, or catastrophic medical events outside insurance.

Use this to understand the shape of your retirement: when does money run out, how sensitive are you to inflation, how much does adding equity help, what's the probability of disaster. Don't use it to decide where to put a specific rupee.

For specific advice, consult a SEBI Registered Investment Adviser (RIA) or a qualified Chartered Accountant.

Can I use this if I'm not in India?

The math (compounding, withdrawal, Monte Carlo, inflation adjustment) is universal. The tax module is hard-coded to India's FY 2025-26 New Regime.

If you're not in India: switch the tax mode to "Flat %" and enter your country's effective rate on interest income. Ignore the SCSS-specific strategy. The other four strategies (FD, 50/50, SWP, Annuity) and all the simulations apply equally — just treat ₹ as your local currency.
